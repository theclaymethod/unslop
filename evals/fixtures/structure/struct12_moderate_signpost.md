Neighborhood pantries often prepare for summer by studying the weeks when school meals stop. A coordinator can compare recent household counts with the same month last year, then separate normal growth from a sudden spike.

Shelf movement gives a second signal. Rice goes first. If beans and diapers disappear before Thursday while canned vegetables remain, the next order should follow that pattern instead of repeating the winter mix.

Driver assignments work better by route than by donor. Shorter loops reduce volunteer fatigue, and predictable pickup windows help churches and schools plan around their own staff.

Storage limits deserve the same attention as demand. Some donations cannot come in. A pantry that cannot refrigerate milk or accept open packages should say so early, before partners collect items the site cannot use or transport safely across town.
