The school garden began as a response to broken pavement behind the cafeteria. Teachers wanted a safer place for science lessons, and parents wanted the lot cleaned before winter.

Students measured the beds, hauled compost, and labeled seedlings with tape that curled in the rain. The first harvest was small, mostly herbs and a few stubborn tomatoes.

The garden later became part of lunch service and after-school tutoring. Younger students learned fractions by dividing seed trays, while older students tracked soil temperature.

Ultimately, the school garden reminds us that shared care can turn broken pavement into a living classroom.
